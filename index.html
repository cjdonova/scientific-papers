How to Read a Scientific Paper

Adapted from Fosmire & Edmondson

1. Why Read Scientific Papers?

Scientific papers are essential because they:

  • Provide the most current and up-to-date research
  • Include raw data and detailed experimental conditions
  • Allow for replication of studies by others
  • Present the authors' logic, assumptions, and conclusions, allowing critical evaluation
  • Support your own research, experiments, and writing
Key insight: Scientific papers are your gateway to the most reliable and current knowledge in any field.

2. A Smarter Reading Strategy

Do not read straight through. It's inefficient and often confusing.
  1. Start with the Abstract
    Offers a concise summary and helps determine if the paper is worth reading in full
  2. Then go to Discussion/Conclusion
    Shows how authors interpret results and relevance to your work
  3. Next, review the Introduction
    Explains the research problem, context, and gaps being addressed
  4. Skim the Results
    Review figures and tables first, check for patterns and trends
  5. Read Methods last (if needed)
    Technical details for replication or reliability assessment

3. Tools for Reading Effectively

  • Use a scientific dictionary for unfamiliar terms
  • Keep a notebook or digital doc for questions and summaries
  • Talk with classmates or instructors about challenging parts
  • Practice active reading by highlighting and annotating

4. Structure of a Scientific Paper

Section Purpose
AbstractSummary of purpose, methods, and findings
IntroductionBackground and research motivation
MethodsStudy design and procedures
ResultsRaw findings with visuals
DiscussionInterpretation and context
ReferencesSources for deeper exploration

5. Reading Figures and Tables

  • Always check axes, scales, and units
  • Identify the variables being compared
  • Ask: What trend is shown? Does it support the conclusion?
  • Read figure captions carefully—they contain key information

6. Signs You've Understood the Paper

You can:

  • Summarize the purpose, methods, and findings
  • Explain how results connect to the research question
  • Identify if the study supports or contradicts other work
  • Decide whether to cite or build upon the article

You're Ready!

You now have a strategic approach to reading scientific papers efficiently and effectively.

Remember the reading order:

  1. Abstract → Get the big picture
  2. Discussion → Understand conclusions
  3. Introduction → Learn the context
  4. Results → Examine the evidence
  5. Methods → Check details (if needed)

Practice this approach with your next scientific paper and watch your comprehension improve dramatically.
